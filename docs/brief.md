# Product Brief

## Product description

CostKit is a Canada-focused analyst workspace for small and mid-sized valuation and family-law CBV firms that brings cost-of-capital support, key data sources, and recurring analyst utilities into one place. Its core promise is accessibility and lower cost versus premium point solutions, with a first wedge around Canadian private-company family-law valuation workflows.

## Six forcing questions

1. **Q: Who is the first paying user, specifically?**  
   **A:** User: Canada-wide family law CBVs working on income for support, financial analysis, CRA valuation, and other valuation work/projects. Buyer: CBV firms. Top recurring jobs: cost of capital tools and related data, industry metrics and benchmarking, life tables, salary classifications, and industry-classification conversion. Current substitutes: no single website; instead Kroll for cost of capital, Statistics Canada and Bank of Canada for public sources, and paid salary-guide websites. Most painful recurring task: collecting information from multiple sources/sites. Why they would pay in the first 30 days: lower-cost access to cost-of-capital tools, cheaper salary-guide access, and eventually related items like CanLII summaries and other analyst utilities in one place.
2. **Q: What is the single wedge product?**  
   **A:** First feature set: a cost-of-capital tool. Workflow replaced: expensive U.S.-based platforms such as Kroll. Point in the day: analysts open it once or twice weekly when working on valuation files. Deliverable improved: not primarily faster output quality yet, but cheaper access to a needed workflow. Why this wedge can expand: many analyst inputs are scattered and expensive; CostKit aims to gather them in one Canada-relevant place using lower-cost and public-source inputs.
3. **Q: Why will a CBV firm trust your cost-of-capital output enough to use it in real work?**  
   **A:** Launch scope expectation: all core cost-of-capital components, including risk-free rate, ERP, beta inputs, industry beta, size premium, specific-company-risk guidance, country risk, cost of debt, capital structure, WACC, and cost of equity. Automation view: none of these should be treated as fully automated final judgment; analyst discretion remains required. Product role: provide multiple data-source options, support the most standard/defensible options, and allow manual overrides. Reliance expectation: analysts may rely on all components in their reports, but supported by transparent research, supporting text, and methodology notes. Credibility story: built by a CBV/valuation-domain operator, focused on Canadian relevance and clear explanation rather than black-box output. Standardization boundary: much of the data gathering and research support can be standardized, but final judgment remains with the analyst.
4. **Q: What exact part of the cost-of-capital workflow becomes materially better than the analyst’s current process, other than price?**  
   **A:** Main value proposition chosen: more accessible. Current workflow: analysts gather source data and studies across many sites, support items like size premium with separate references, and sometimes manually override with judgment. Claimed bottleneck: not a single sharp bottleneck, but fragmented data and tools. Claimed 10x improvement: smaller firms that cannot justify premium subscriptions can still access a usable all-in-one analyst website, saving time and reducing tool fragmentation. Longer-term extension: potentially support valuation-report sections using AI later.
5. **Q: What is the first narrow use case where a small Canadian CBV firm would switch behavior immediately?**  
   **A:** Beachhead file type: a family-law valuation of a private company. First users inside the firm: analyst, manager, and partner. Output needed: both a number and, ideally, a snapshot/export and at times Excel output. If they do not have Kroll today: they may assume a number based on judgment. Why change behavior this month: the pitch is lower cost plus ease of use in one place, which should be enough to drive repeat use if the first experience is good.
6. **Q: Why does this become a real business instead of a useful side tool?**  
   **A:** Proposed pricing direction: per firm, with access by seat; some features free and others paid. Freemium examples: limited salary information free, advanced salary data paid; basic classification-code lookup free, advanced classification-from-description paid. Business thesis: become the default working page for small Canadian valuation firms by packaging many scattered tools and data sources into one affordable site, then deepen into recurring workflows, exports, saved projects, and eventually richer report support.

## Success metrics

- **North Star:** Weekly Active Output Accounts (WAOA) = number of firm accounts that generate **3 or more substantive analyst outputs in a rolling 7-day period**. Substantive outputs include a cost-of-capital snapshot, Excel export, methodology note/report-ready summary, or premium classification/salary analysis.
- **Supporting:**
  - **Activation rate:** % of new firm seats that generate **1 cost-of-capital output + 1 export within the first 7 days**. Target: 40%+ for invited seats in paying firms; 25%+ for self-serve/free signups.
  - **7-day repeat usage rate:** % of activated users who return and generate a **second substantive output on a different day within 7 days**. Target: 50%+.
  - **Multi-seat adoption per firm:** % of active firm accounts with **2 or more seats generating substantive outputs in the same 30-day period**. Target: 60%+ for paying firms.

- **Activation event:** A new user is activated when, within 7 days of signup, they **(1) create or open a project/file, (2) generate one cost-of-capital result or comparable premium analysis output, and (3) export or copy a usable artifact** such as Excel, PDF snapshot, methodology note, or saved output.
- **Revenue metric:** **New Weekly ARR Added** = annualized value of all new paid subscriptions, upgrades, and seat expansions added that week, split into New Logo ARR and Expansion ARR.
- **Churn indicator:** **2-week output collapse flag** for any paying firm where substantive outputs fall by **50% or more over 14 days** versus the prior 14 days, or where no second seat is active in the last 14 days, or where no export/save/copy artifact event occurs in the last 14 days.

## Pricing research

### Competitor list

- **Kroll Cost of Capital Navigator**  
  Positioning: premium, authoritative cost-of-capital platform with strong methodology brand, exports, and valuation-market trust.  
  Pricing: public site does not clearly show a simple public sticker price; effectively non-free, premium, and appears quote/check-out based.  
  Free tier: none meaningful.
- **BVR Cost of Capital Professional**  
  Positioning: subscription cost-of-capital platform for valuation professionals with lower public visibility than Kroll.  
  Pricing: current public exact price not clearly verified in-thread; treated as subscription / non-free.  
  Free tier: none meaningful.
- **ERI Salary Assessor**  
  Positioning: deep compensation benchmarking and salary data platform.  
  Pricing: user estimate in thread was roughly **US$3,000–US$4,000/year**; public exact sticker price was not cleanly verified in-thread.  
  Free tier: no meaningful free tier; demos/guided access only.
- **Payscale**  
  Positioning: compensation intelligence platform for salary benchmarking and pay workflows.  
  Pricing: user estimate in thread was roughly **US$12,000–US$35,000/year** for larger organizations; public pricing is typically quote-based rather than transparent self-serve.  
  Free tier: limited/free salary-data access exists, but not a full free working tier.
- **Classification.Codes**  
  Positioning: paid correspondence/crosswalk tables for industry/classification-code conversion.  
  Pricing: user estimate in thread was **€1,759 + VAT** for a full conversion/correspondence file example.  
  Free tier: limited sample/free lookup content only.
- **Free substitutes**  
  Positioning: raw official/public sources such as Bank of Canada, Statistics Canada, public concordances, and other government/public resources.  
  Pricing: free.  
  Weakness: fragmented, not packaged into valuation workflow, and no report-ready workspace/export layer.

### Chosen pricing direction

Three plausible pricing structures were developed:

1. **Firm subscription + seat caps**  
   Example: Free tier, then **C$59/month** for a small-seat plan, **C$99/month** for a broader firm plan, plus enterprise/custom later.  
   Trade-off: best fit with small-firm buying, but risks underpricing if a firm derives high value across many files.
2. **Per-seat SaaS pricing**  
   Example: **C$29/seat/month** basic and **C$49/seat/month** pro.  
   Trade-off: clean unit economics, but more friction for small firms that want broad internal access.
3. **Hybrid: low platform fee + paid modules/exports**  
   Example: free utilities, then a low firm platform fee plus paid premium modules like salary analyzer, classification intelligence, exports, or saved projects.  
   Trade-off: fits the broad-site vision well, but adds pricing complexity.

### Recommended starting price

Recommended initial posture:

- **Free tier:** public data pages, simple yields/FX/tax pages, limited calculator runs/lookups, and little or no export.
- **Pro Firm:** **C$79/month** for up to about 5 seats, including cost-of-capital workspace, exports, saved projects, methodology/source trail, and broader analyst utilities.
- **Annual Pro Firm:** **C$790/year**.
- Later possible higher tier: **C$139/month** Team / Advanced.

Reasoning: this is still far below premium enterprise alternatives, preserves a professional positioning, and leaves room for expansion tiers without feeling like a hobby-price product.

## NOT BUILDING (v1 scope limit)

- Full AI-generated valuation report drafting or full report-section generation.
- CanLII case summaries and legal-research workflow as a core v1 module.
- Full marketplace or multi-vendor data marketplace model.
- Custom enterprise integrations with firm internal systems or DMS software.
- Fully automated final WACC / cost-of-capital conclusions with no analyst judgment.
- Broad salary platform depth matching enterprise compensation products.
- Large-scale workflow for non-CBV audiences outside the initial valuation/family-law use case.
- Too many equal-priority tools at launch; v1 should not try to build 20 tools to the same depth.
- A broad “everything for finance professionals” platform positioning.
- Complex multi-country support beyond the initial Canadian focus.
